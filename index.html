Theatre Positions

Here are some positions I've held in organizations that I'm particularly proud of.

Documentation Intern, Katkatha Puppet Arts Trust, New Delhi, August 2016

Katkatha is a puppet theatre group in India. Their contemporary form of puppetry is based on the principle that the puppeteer can breathe life into any object and turn it into a puppet. At the same time, they draw from Bunraku, Togalu Gombeyaata, Kalaripayattu, and other traditional techniques of puppetry and movement.

I worked with them the summer after graduating from college and helped them document the development of their newest show, an adaptaion of the Mahabharata. I shadowed their rehearsals; learned about puppetry, movement, folklore; took photographs; wrote a blog for them; and designed a website for them.

Read the blog in which I compare them to Cézanne.

Chair, PenNaatak Theatre, University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, 2014-2015 and 2015-2016 seasons

Being on the board of a theatre group for three years (chair for two) gave me a lot of insight into the work it takes to actually put a show together. The vision I had for creating a space to bring new shows from all around the globe to Penn brought us to open Naatak's mission statement up. By the time I graduated, PenNaatak went from being Penn's South Asian theatre group to Penn's only Global Contemporary theatre group.

Board Member, One Acts Festival, University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, 2014 and 2015

Penn's One Acts Festival was a great platform to encourage people to try new things and I was happy to be a part of it as Tech Director in 2014 and as Vice Chair and Assistant Producer in 2015.

Co-founder, Paper Faces Theatre, Kolkata, 2011

I took a year off before heading to college and worked with two extremely talented friends to start a little theatre company in Kolkata. Together, we produced and directed two shows: Stephen Sondheim's Sweeney Todd in 2011 and Neel Chaudhuri's Taramandal in 2012.

Direction

Director, Red by John Logan, Philadelphia, April 2016

Easily the show I'm most proud of having worked on, Red was an experiment in realism and intimacy. I wanted to talk about art without distancing the viewer from the creation and the creative process. So, with a team of wonderfully talented actors and designers, we invited the audience into Penn's wood shop, which we turned into Mark Rothko's studio.

Working in a four-person rehearsal room gave us the flexibility to really dig in to character early on in the process. We worked to build a general narrative arc, and specific character arcs. As the director, I then helped usher all the elements to come together and contribute to that vision. If we had conersations about the cyclic nature of art, I'd think of how that could be incorporated in the physicality of the actors, in the lighting changes, in the costumes.

Scenic Design

Scenic Designer, The Who & The What by Ayad Akhtar, Philadelphia, Spring 2015

Ayad Akhtar's show about a Pakistani family in Atlanta made me think about the art and architecture choices of the father, a middle-aged Pakistani immigrant, versus the younger Eli, a white Muslim convert raised on Malcolm X's teachings. This made me explore and contrast their generational and class differences through the scenic design.

Scenic Designer, Tara by Mahesh Dattani, Philadelphia, Fall 2015

I for some reason never found the time to take pictures of this set, although it was one of my favorites. A corrupt administration represented by a doctor always on stage was given a ten-foot high pedestal to tower over the characters stuck in a Kafkaesque system. The floor was painted with a false shadow of the wooden frame of the ten-foot platform. The doctor has left after the surgery to seperate the twins, but the scars can never leave the life of the characters Tara and Chandan.

Scenic Designer and Stage Manager, Bengal Tiger at the Baghdad Zoo by Rajiv Joseph, Philadelphia, Spring 2014

Rajiv Joseph is one of my favorite playwrights. The director wanted to talk about the spirituality and sanctity of human life within this war torn city. The scenic design represented that by putting a carpeted mosque-like are within elements of grime, concrete, graffitied walls.
